Where does Dale Steyn rank?

Published: Tue 04 March 2014
By dsaihurrish

In Cricket.

Part I – Among his contemporaries

Dale Steyn has now played in 71 test matches picking up 361 wickets at an average of 22.89. This is indeed a great achievement. It has also happened in an era when most bowlers average around 30. But how awesome is Dale Steyn? Has he always been this awesome? Does anyone else come near? Does his awesomeness have to do with where he plays most of his cricket? In search for these answers I took to Cricinfo Statsguru. From there, I downloaded Match Totals (Overs/Maidens/Runs/Wickets) by Bowler for every test match (Ground / Opposition and Start Date uniquely identified test match) between Dec 17 2004 (Dale Steyn’s Debut) – Feb 20 2014 (Steyn’s last game). To exclude rare part-timers whose figures provide no value in predicting Bowler success, I used a rather arbitrary criterion of Bowlers with more than 20 Overs per Match. This left me with 245 bowlers of whom 167 were classified as Pace Bowlers.

First, I plotted the distribution of bowling averages by Year. There are obviously some outliers (avg. > 200) and 2004 and 2014 include only about a month of data. Excluding these two years, the Median Bowling Average (solid blue line) ranges from 29.6 (in 2013) to 34.9 (in 2006). It depresses me to think that the median price of a wicket in test cricket would be 35. On the bright side, Ishant Sharma would have had a realistic chance of being below the 50^th^ percentile. The 25^th^ and 75^th^ percentiles (dashed blue lines) ranged from 22.7 (2013) -28 (2009) and 39.9 (2013) – 50.4 (2006) respectively. Dale Steyn has been around the 25^th^ percentile (solid red line), having his best year in 2006 – 49 wickets at 16.53, and worst year in 2011 – 22 wickets at 29.86.

Among all bowlers in his era who played in 10 or more matches and picked up 50 or more wickets, Steyn has the fourth-best average.

This is the story told by crude averages. But as noted earlier, there are factors that play a role in determining bowling averages. Dare I say that if Steyn played most of his cricket in India, he would have different figures? What if we gave them our two Sharmas, R Ashwin and a future left-arm spinner from the Ranji circuit to find out – fair trade? No? But to find out, I employed some statistical methods. Firstly, I used a regression model to predict how many wickets a bowler would pick up in a match using Opposition (Bangladesh are a test team), Ground or Venue (Mitch at WACA is like Murali in a Kandy store), Home or Away (ask R Ashwin), Balls Bowled (more you bowl, more you get - unless you’re bowling to Faf on day 5) and Bowler Type (Pace or Spin) as predictors (Model fit: G^2^=13246, df=43285, p-value=0.999 = goood!). I also let the effects of Grounds the match was played on to vary depending on the type of Bowler which I think is a justifiable assumption.This model gave me the probabilities of a bowler picking up anywhere between 0-13 wickets in each match, using which I calculated the expected number of wickets. I stopped estimating probabilities at 13 wickets as the largest tally of wickets in this time period was 13 by M Ntini. Then, I fit another regression model to predict the number of runs a bowler would concede in a match using the same predictors (Model fit: R^2^=0.68 = good!).

Now, using the predicted (or expected) wickets and runs for each bowler in a match I arrived at expected averages (=Predicted Runs / Predicted Wickets). So, for each bowler I had an Observed (Crude) and an Expected (Predicted) average. I used the ratio of Observed / Expected Averages as a measure of bowler performance, with a smaller ratio indicating superior performance - just like bowling averages. By this measure, Dale Steyn’s performance does not stand out as much but still stands out. He comes in ninth among all bowlers who played in 10 or more matches and picked up 50 or more wickets and fourth among active players (minus Amir,bring back Amir ). A big surprise here is Ryan Harris who by this measure appears to be highly underrated. Philander has been phenomenal but I expect to see some regression to the mean. However, no other bowler in his era seems to have enjoyed the consistent success that Dale Steyn has. By the O/E measure Dale Steyn has been around the 25^th^ percentile, doing worse than expected only once, in ’05 (O/E of 1.04), and managing fantastic returns in 2006. when everyone else seemed to struggle, and 2013 (O/E of 0.65 and 0.61 respectively).

So, Steyn’s a beast but not perfect. I’m glad I didn’t spend two days figuring out what I could have learnt from looking at his career info page at Cricinfo. It should be interesting to see how Steyn’s career achievements compare with that of former greats like Marshall, Mcgrath, Lillee, Waqar and Wasim using the metric discussed here.
