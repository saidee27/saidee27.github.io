College experience and NBA-readiness: an analysis of first-round draft picks' performance

Thu 24 July 2014 | tags: NBA, NBA draft, -- (permalink)

Recently, Nate Silver wrote about drafting sophomores being a ‘Smart’ strategy in the NBA draft. As Silver points out, rookie scale contracts are valid for four years, with team options for the third and fourth year i.e., a team can get rid of draft picks after their second or third season, or have to decide on offering them an extension around the start of the fourth year. Rookie-scale contracts provide the most wins for money and good teams (should) capitalize on this by making well-researched picks. For lottery and early first round draft picks, while most of them are likely to get really good by the fourth year, they also get really expensive at that point. While, teams which draft the player can offer him the maximum money, it may not be a bad idea to get a lot more cheap wins in the first four years given that there's no guarantee that the player won't take his talents elsewhere. Teams with mid-late first round picks should maybe look to draft players who peak early in their careers and since these players are not likely to have much upside, teams can afford (or maybe should) not to offer them a max contract at the end of the fourth year. Apart from getting you more wins for less money, players who peak early may also be of help for late first round drafting teams, which are usually contenders, in their quest for a title. Think Rondo, Leonard and not the Human Victory Cigar.

What Nate Silver found was that sophomores produced more wins in the first five years of their careers than any other group of players. I decided to check this out myself. So, I went about gathering data (from basketball-reference.com) on first round (top 30) draft picks from 1998 – 2013 and looked at Win Shares produced^1^. As expected, for the first 5 years of their career sophomores had more Win Shares than juniors, freshmen / high school, international players. Although, freshmen and high schoolers seem to distance themselves from the other groups after five years, sophomores and juniors seem to be getting the better of them in the first four years with respect to number of wins produced. While both sophomores and juniors seem to grow faster than freshmen/HS in this period, sophomores also seem to start higher - produce more wins in the first year then freshmen and juniors.

[=

For now, let’s concentrate on the first four years. Through the first four years of their career sophomores produced 10.1 wins, which was 0.4 wins more than freshmen and high schoolers who produced 9.6 wins. You can see this in the plot below. You’ll also notice that this is mainly due to sophomore (and junior) Guards outperforming freshmen and high school Guards through the first 4 years of their NBA career. Indeed, freshmen/HS centers did much better than sophomore Centers and there was not much of a difference when in came to Forwards.

It is worth noting here that I did not find out any of these differences to be statistically significant at the .05 level (i.e., if it were true that the number of wins produced was the same for all groups, there is more than a 5 % chance that we would have observed differences at least as extreme as what we did observe). But, this difference is not adjusted for Draft Pick position. My guess was that since freshmen and high schoolers tend to go higher in the draft the differences are biased toward the null i.e., I expected the differences between sophomore and freshmen / HS Guards to be significant after controlling for the Draft Pick position. Another factor I thought needed to be adjusted for was the number of games played. The more you play the more wins you produce. It is worth mentioning here that I looked at the first four years after which the player was drafted regardless of when they actually started playing. For example, Blake Griffin’s first season was 2009-10.

So, after controlling for number of games played and Pick position, I found that both sophomores and juniors produced 4.6 [95% Confidence Interval: 1.6 – 7.6] wins more than freshmen and high schoolers through their first 4 years. Surprisingly, I found that, controlling for the number of games played, even seniors did significantly better than freshmen and high schoolers picked at the same position in the draft producing an additional 3.9 [95% CI: 1.0 – 6.9] wins through their first 4 years. Further, most seniors (87% or 44/51) and juniors (75% or 35/47) were drafted beyond the top 10, while exactly half of the freshmen/HS to play in the NBA were drafted in the top 10. So, while from the figure above it looks like freshmen/HS are at least producing more wins than seniors in their first four years, given that they were mostly picked higher they should be producing a lot more wins. This indicates that freshmen / HS guards are perhaps overvalued, seniors undervalued and juniors and sophomores seriously undervalued. It may also be that teams are not that concerned about the production in the first 4 years. This might be a mistake - because how do you know who’s worth a max?

For Forwards and Centers, as expected, the freshmen/HS group does not seem to be significantly better than any of the other groups with respect to wins produced after controlling for draft pick position. While that means that there is no significant advantage in picking freshmen or high school Forwards to be had in the short term (over the first 4 years), it might just be the better strategy in the long term as freshmen/HS players seem to have a better upside after the first four years.

Now, for guards, I wanted to see if there was any difference in the trajectory or the rate at which sophomores, juniors and freshmen improved during their first four years. The figure below makes me think that this might be the case.

So, I fit a linear model to predict a player’s win shares in each of his first four years in the league and tested if the yearly change in wins produced (rate of growth) was different for sophomores, freshmen/HS, juniors, internationals and seniors^2^. As I did previously, I controlled for the effect of the draft pick position and the number of games played.  I also controlled for the effect of Win-Loss percent of the team in which the player played that season^3,4^. Doing so, gave me a good comparison of the rates of growth in each category and an interesting pattern emerged. I found that the more years a player spent in college the earlier he peaked or had a growth spurt. In the first year seniors produced more wins than any other group. This translated to 1.03 [95% CI: 0.1 – 1.95] more wins produced than freshmen/HS guards in the first year. Thereafter, from year 1 to year 2, Juniors had the highest increase in Win shares, growing at +1.13 [95% CI: 0.36 – 1.92] more wins than freshmen/HS. Finally, from year 1 to year 3 and year 1 to year 4, Sophomores showed the largest growth in wins produced. From year 1 to year 4, sophomores grew at +1.3 [95% CI: 0.14 – 2.46] more wins than freshmen/HS.

The relationship between NBA-readiness and the number of years spent in college can be easily demonstrated by looking at the best players with respect to first year production, second year growth and third year growth. Outside of the top 10 whom we might expect to do very well in the first year and where seniors are seldom picked, the best performance in terms of Win Shares comes from Courtney Lee, a Senior picked 22nd in the 2008, who produced 4.4 win shares. The next best player picked beyond 10 was Tony Parker with 3.8 win shares. In terms of second year growth, four of the top five players in this category were Juniors. Here’s the top five:

  1. Baron Davis, Sophomore, drafted 3^rd^ in 1999: +6.8 win shares
  2. Vince Carter, Junior, drafted 5^th^ in 1998: +6.8 win shares
  3. Dwayne Wade, Junior, drafted 5^th^ in 2003: +6.7 win shares
  4. Ronnie Brewer, Junior, drafted 14^th^ in 2006: +6.1 win shares
  5. Steve Francis, Junior, drafted 2^nd^ in 1999: +5.8 win shares

And when it comes to improvement from year 1 to year 3, four of the top 5 players are sophomores. The top five were:

  1. Gilbert Arenas, Sophomore, drafted 30^th^ in 2001: +8.8 win shares
  2. James Harden, Sophomore, drafted 3^rd^ in 2009: +8.3 win shares
  3. Kevin Martin, Junior, drafted 26^th^ in 2004: +8.3 win shares
  4. Chris Paul, Sophomore, drafted 4^th^ in 2005: +7.9 win shares
  5. Rajon Rondo, Sophomore, drafted 21^st^ in 2006: +7.2 win shares

So, it is fairly clear that the more time you spend in college the more ready you are for the NBA. And depending on how early a team wants to get the most out of its' pick in the first four years and how long it intends to keep the player, the smartest strategy might be to pick seniors, juniors or sophomores. What remains to be seen is if after year four or five, freshmen/HS guards show a higher growth rate than all the other groups and perhaps eventually reach a higher ceiling, which I suspect they would, and if so what is the difference in terms of long term wins produced. From the plot above, it seems like sophomores, juniors and freshmen/HS are pretty much at the same level at year 10. If this remains the case after adjusting for pick position, drafting sophomore and junior guards might be the best thing to do. Of course, international players are tough to predict and their growth, among other things, might depend on the nature and amount of previous basketball experience.

FOOTNOTES

  1. This included 117 seniors, 106 juniors, 86 sophomores, 90 freshmen/HS (66 freshmen + 24 HS) and 69 international players.
  2. Among guards there were 51 seniors, 51 juniors, 36 sophomores, 36 freshmen/HS and 21 international players.
  3. The model I used was:\ Win Shares \~ Pk + Year in league + College experience (SR/JR/SO/FHS/INT.) + College experience*Year in league + Games played + Win% of team.
  4. Yes, I adjusted for correlation between multiple observations from the same player by specifying an unstructured covariance structure.
